The Problem

A classification system built for common variants

Clinical genomics has a frequency problem. Existing tools use population allele frequency and prior case databases to classify variants — a methodology that systematically fails for rare disease.

When a variant appears fewer than five times in gnomAD or ClinVar, classification algorithms default to "Variant of Uncertain Significance" — a label that functionally means "we don't know." That label ends the diagnostic journey for most patients.

Whole-genome and exome sequencing are now routine, yet each sequencing run returns 500–1,000 candidate variants with no mechanistic prioritisation. Clinicians are handed a spreadsheet when they need a ranked differential. Manual expert review of each candidate can take weeks — and expertise in every affected gene simply doesn't exist.

How GeneRank Works

Structure-first prioritisation — not frequency matching

We evaluate variants the way a biochemist would: by asking what they do to the protein. Two complementary engines work in tandem to reduce a candidate list from hundreds to a clinically actionable handful.

🧬

AlphaFold-Powered Structure Prediction

Each candidate variant is mapped onto a full 3D protein model generated using AlphaFold2. We evaluate predicted local distance difference test (pLDDT) scores and per-residue confidence before running mutation-specific folding simulations to detect structural perturbations — without relying on prior case data.

⚛️

Molecular Dynamics & Binding Affinity Scoring

Shortlisted structures are subjected to molecular dynamics simulations to assess thermodynamic stability (ΔΔG), active-site geometry, and ligand-binding disruption. Variants that destabilise protein conformation beyond a biophysical threshold are flagged as high-priority candidates — regardless of whether similar mutations have been catalogued before.

👥

HPO-Based Phenotype Similarity Matching

Remaining candidates are scored against the patient's Human Phenotype Ontology (HPO) profile and cross-referenced against international rare disease registries including Orphanet, OMIM, and disease-specific cohort databases. Semantic similarity scoring surfaces gene–disease relationships that frequency-based tools cannot detect.

The Science

Why protein structure is the missing signal

Frequency-based tools ask "have we seen this before?" Structure-based tools ask "what does this change do to the protein?" For rare variants, only the second question has an answer.

AlphaFold2 as a diagnostic substrate

DeepMind's AlphaFold2 predicts protein structure at near-experimental accuracy for virtually every human protein. GeneRank uses it not just to model wild-type structure, but to model both the reference and each mutant sequence — then computes structural divergence at the residue level.

Variants that produce large pLDDT drops in functional domains, disrupted β-sheet geometry, or lost secondary structure at known binding interfaces are scored as structurally damaging — a classification that doesn't require a single prior clinical case.

Molecular dynamics beyond static snapshots

Static structure alone doesn't capture protein behaviour. Our GPU-accelerated MD pipeline runs nanosecond-scale simulations to measure thermodynamic stability changes (ΔΔG via FoldX + Rosetta ensemble scoring), conformational drift, and active-site RMSD under physiological conditions.

This eliminates candidates that appear structurally plausible at rest but become unstable at body temperature — a frequent source of false positives in static-model pipelines.

The GeneRank Pipeline

Five stages from VCF to verdict

1
Variant Ingestion
Accepts VCF, gVCF, BAM, CRAM, or FASTQ. Variants are normalised, annotated with Ensembl VEP, and filtered against population-frequency databases (gnomAD, 1000 Genomes).
2
AlphaFold Structure Modelling
Each candidate is modelled using AlphaFold2 on both wild-type and mutant sequences. pLDDT confidence scores and predicted aligned error (PAE) matrices flag structurally significant changes.
3
Biophysical Simulation
Shortlisted variants undergo molecular dynamics simulation. ΔΔG folding stability, active-site geometry deviation, and protein–protein interface disruption are scored to eliminate structurally implausible candidates.
4
Phenotype Ontology Matching
The patient's HPO terms are semantically matched against OMIM, Orphanet, and curated gene–disease associations. Remaining candidates are re-ranked by phenotypic concordance score.
5
Clinical Report
5–10 prioritised variants delivered with ACMG-aligned evidence summaries, confidence intervals, supporting literature, and suggested next diagnostic steps — same day.
